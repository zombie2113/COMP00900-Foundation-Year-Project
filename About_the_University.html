History of the University

To begin with the University of Manchester was first established in the 1800s where it was granted a Royal Charter in becoming England's first civic university it was renamed the Victoria University of Manchester in 1903. But the current University of Manchester was created on the 22 nd of October 2004 by the amalgamation of the Victoria University of Manchester and the University of Manchester Institute of Science and Technology (UMIST). This was decided by both institutes after working closely together for approximately one hundred years, creating the largest single-site university in the UK. (It has the second highest number of students enrolled, the open university is the first.)


Victoria University of Manchester

The Victoria University of Manchester was first developed out of the Owens College which was founded in the year 1851, where it further expanded from 1872 where a medical school (owned by doctors) known as the Royal School of Medicine and Surgery, was incorporated into Victoria UoM. Moreover, Owens college was created by Johns Owens in 1845 (where construction first began) which was part of his legacy, which was to open a college for education and being done so on non-sectarian lines
From the 1860s a new vision came about from the college, the leading professors looked to the German Universities for inspiration. This is where they learned that the key for progression is to invest more time and money into research as it advances the knowledge of mankind and has many major benefits.


UMIST

UMIST (which stands for The University of Manchester Institute of Science and Technology) was founded in 1824 as part of a movement which aimed to forward the education of working men in this era. This idea was put forward because very few people had basic education; mainly because primary schooling education was not yet compulsory, this however changed on 1870.

In addition to this UMIST was growing in a rapid way and needed a new building too keep up with the added number of students and this thus in 1853 a new building was opened on Princess Street. At the time Britain was facing fierce competition for the top spot in being an industrial nation, this then lead ex-shoemaker, John Henry Reynolds to run a programme of classes which primarily focused on the industrial needs of Manchester. In 1883 he then decided to convert the Mechanics' Institute into the Manchester Technical School. Finally, from the year 1892 the Technical school became officially funded by the Manchester Corporation, and the school now became Municipal Technical School.


Buildings

There are many notable buildings at the University, including the Sackville Street Building, which was was opened by the Municipal Technical School in 1902, but after the first world war the school was renamed to the Manchester Municipal College of Technology.

The Samuel Alexander building, named after Samuel Alexander, Professor of Philosophy (1859-1925). The building currently houses most of the School of Arts, languages and cultures theology. Samuel Alexander was a philosopher, originally from Melbourne Australia who then moved to Oxford to study philosophy. But, then became a professor of philosophy in Manchester from 1893 until his death. The reason why he was so important was due to the fact he supported women's rights and advocated for their education


Nobel Prize Winners

The University of Manchester has 25 Nobel laureates to its name, including former staff and students. Some of these prize winners include the likes of Ernest Rutherford who was awarded a Nobel Prize in Chemistry (1908) for discovering the atomic structure. He essentially fired alpha particles at a thin sheet of gold, however roughly 1 in 8000 particles deflected backwards, leading too the conclusion the atom has a small area with a concentrated piece of mass (which we now know as the nucleus.) James Chadwick was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics (1935) shortly after Rutherford\u2019s discovery, but Chadwick did not discover the atomic structure, he instead discovered the existence of neutrons. (Chadwick and Rutherford used to work together). A more recent example of a novel prize winner is for physics in 2010 by Konstantin Novoselov. He essentially discovered the properties of graphene by playing around in a lab session.


Courses at the University

Currently the University of Manchester offers hundreds of courses ranging from Accounting all the way too zoology, most degree programmes offer an opportunity to achieve a masters, or PHD and a few offering a year in industry or a placement year. The University also offers the chance for students to continue with some research even after graduating at the university. But one major thing that differentiates the University of Manchester from many other Universities is that it has such a diverse range of students. In fact students from about 160 countries are currently enrolled here.

The History of the School of Computer Science

The School of Computer Science at the University of Manchester is one of the oldest Computer Science schools in the UK. Across the years, it has had 9 Heads of School and collectively the department has most certainly contributed greatly to the development of Computer Science. To celebrate the 50th Anniversary of the School of Computer Science in 2014, several statistics were compiled to showcase the achievements of the school.

Despite the School being founded in 1964, Computer Science was first introduced to the University of Manchester in 1946 when Tom Kilburn and Frederic Calland Williams started developing the cathode ray tube which later became the first Random Access Memory device. Throughout 1967, Kilburn and Williams worked together on the 'Baby', the world's first device considered to be a computer. Various computers were developed then, with the assistance of Dr Alan Turing who had joined the Department of Mathematics in October 1948. Throughout the following years, various faster computers and high-level computer languages were developed by the team, incluhttp://www.cs.manchester.ac.uk/about-us/staff/ding the Ferranti Atlas.


The focus of the School was on engineering, software and mathematics. It grew in popularity, opening its £1.7M purpose-build building, the Kilburn building, in 1972. Since then the department has grown and grown, being involved in many projects including Amulet 1, based around developing commercial microprocessors, and MAVERIK, one of the first VR systems and text mining. Nowadays, the department is researching a number of projects.

Learning at the School of Computer Science

Students taking a degree in the School of Computer Science aren't necessarily taking a Computer Science degree, there are a few different pathways for students to take. Undergraduate, Postgraduate and Taught Master's positions are available at the School. Artificial Intelligence, Computer Science, Computer Systems Engineering, Computer Science and Mathematics, Human Computer Interaction and Software Engineering degrees are available for Undergraduates interested in studying at the University. Three, four and five-year courses are offered, some with Industrial Experience placements available for successful students. Placements could be with companies such as Google, Microsoft or Electronic Arts to name a few. Each of the courses has its own separate modules, for which information can be found on the website of the School which was linked earlier.
